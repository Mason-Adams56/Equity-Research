# Microsoft Corporation (MSFT) - Research Note

**Date:** July 20, 2026
**Analyst:** Mason
**Rating framework:** Constructive (educational, not a recommendation)

## 1. Business Overview

Microsoft is a diversified software and cloud company operating in three segments: Productivity and Business Processes (Microsoft 365, LinkedIn, Dynamics), Intelligent Cloud (Azure, server products, GitHub), and More Personal Computing (Windows, Search advertising, Xbox, Devices). The business model is dominated by recurring subscription and consumption revenue sold to enterprises, which makes revenue unusually predictable for a company of this size.

In fiscal Q3 2026 (quarter ended March 31, 2026), revenue was $82.9 billion, up 18% year over year, with EPS of $4.27, up 21% adjusted for the OpenAI investment impact. Microsoft Cloud revenue was $54.5 billion, up 29%, meaning roughly two thirds of the company is now cloud.

## 2. Industry and Competitive Position

Azure competes with AWS and Google Cloud in a three-player oligopoly where all participants are capacity constrained by data center buildout. Microsoft's edge is distribution: it sells AI and cloud into an installed enterprise base that already runs on Microsoft 365 and Windows Server, which lowers customer acquisition cost relative to rivals. Switching costs across the productivity suite remain the core moat.

## 3. Thesis

**Driver 1: Azure growth is durable, not a one-time AI spike.** Azure grew 40% in Q3 FY26 against a prior year that was itself accelerating, and management guided Q4 Azure growth to 39-40% in constant currency. Consumption growth across both AI and non-AI services suggests workload migration is broadening, not narrowing. Metric to watch: Azure constant currency growth staying above 35%.

**Driver 2: The market is mispricing the capex debate.** Capital expenditures and finance leases were $31.9 billion in the quarter, up 49%, with roughly $190 billion planned for calendar 2026. Gross margin has compressed to about 68%, the lowest since 2022, on data center depreciation. The bear case treats this as margin destruction; the bull case treats it as prepaid revenue given Azure is supply constrained. If Azure growth holds while depreciation flows through, operating income growth (up 20% this quarter) proves the spend is earning its return. Metric to watch: operating margin versus Azure growth in the same quarter.

**Driver 3: Copilot is becoming a real per-seat business.** Microsoft 365 Copilot passed 20 million commercial seats, up from 15 million in January. At scale this is high-margin attach revenue on an existing sales motion. Metric to watch: sequential seat adds and M365 commercial cloud growth (19% this quarter).

## 4. Recent Results (FY26 Q3, ended March 31, 2026)

- Revenue $82.9B, up 18% (15% constant currency)
- Net income $31.78B; EPS $4.27 vs $3.46 prior year
- Azure and other cloud services up 40% (39% cc)
- Microsoft Cloud $54.5B, up 29%; cloud gross margin 66%, down YoY on AI infrastructure investment
- Capex plus finance leases $31.9B, up 49%
- Q4 FY26 guidance: revenue $86.7B to $87.8B; implied operating margin ~44%
- Returned $10.2B to shareholders in the quarter

## 5. Valuation

Preferred method: 10-year DCF (see DCF_Model_Template.xlsx in /templates). The debate lives in three assumptions: revenue growth rate as Azure scales (I model low-to-mid teens consolidated growth fading toward high single digits), the margin path as AI capex depreciates through cost of revenue, and the reinvestment rate implied by roughly $190B of annual capex. At current prices the market is paying a premium multiple to the S&P, so the burden of proof is on growth durability. Build the model, then flex Azure growth down 10 points and see how much of the value survives. That sensitivity is the actual investment question.

## 6. Risks

1. **AI capex outruns demand.** If Azure decelerates while $190B of capex converts to depreciation, margins compress with no offsetting revenue. Early warning: Azure growth below guidance two quarters running.
2. **Gross margin erosion becomes structural.** Cloud gross margin is already down YoY; efficiency gains need to keep pace with buildout.
3. **OpenAI relationship complexity.** Investment accounting already swings reported EPS; strategic divergence would be a bigger issue.
4. **Regulatory pressure** on bundling and market power in the US and EU.

## 7. Conclusion

Microsoft is the rare mega-cap where the growth story and the risk story are the same line item: AI infrastructure spend. The Q3 print showed both at once, with 40% Azure growth and the lowest gross margin in four years. I lean constructive because supply-constrained demand plus distribution advantages usually resolve in the incumbent's favor, but the position of this thesis lives or dies on the capex-to-revenue conversion. Next catalyst: FY26 Q4 earnings and FY27 capex guidance.
