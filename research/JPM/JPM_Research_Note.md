# JPMorgan Chase & Co. (JPM) - Research Note

**Date:** July 20, 2026
**Analyst:** Mason
**Rating framework:** Neutral to constructive (educational, not a recommendation)

## 1. Business Overview

JPMorgan Chase is the largest US bank by assets, operating across Consumer & Community Banking (Chase retail, cards), the Corporate & Investment Bank (markets, investment banking, payments), Commercial Banking, and Asset & Wealth Management. Earnings come from two engines: net interest income (the spread earned on loans and deposits) and noninterest revenue (trading, investment banking fees, asset management fees). The diversification across those engines is the core of the franchise, since they tend to offset across rate and market cycles.

## 2. Industry and Competitive Position

US banking is scale-driven, and JPMorgan is the scale leader with a top position in nearly every business it runs. Its "fortress balance sheet" and deposit franchise give it a funding cost advantage over regional banks, and its technology budget exceeds what most competitors earn in total profit. Main competitors: Bank of America and Citigroup across the franchise, Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley in markets and banking. Note for interview purposes: bank financial statements differ fundamentally from industrials, since the balance sheet IS the business, and banks are analyzed on ROTCE, net interest margin, credit costs, and capital ratios rather than EBITDA.

## 3. Thesis

**Driver 1: Structurally higher ROTCE than the market gives credit for.** Q2 2026 adjusted ROTCE was 23% (29% including one-time gains). A bank that compounds tangible book value at 20%+ deserves a premium to tangible book, and the durable question is whether normalized ROTCE sits near 20% or reverts toward 17%. Metric to watch: ROTCE excluding significant items across the next four quarters.

**Driver 2: Fee engines are firing at the same time as NII grows.** Q2 equity markets revenue rose 86% to $6.0 billion, investment banking fees rose 30% to $3.3 billion (highest since 2021), and AWM assets under management hit $5.1 trillion, up 18%. Meanwhile full-year NII guidance was raised to about $105.5 billion. Both engines growing at once is unusual and explains the earnings beat. The risk is that markets revenue at this level is cyclical peak, not run rate.

**Driver 3: Capital return capacity.** Dividend raised to $1.65 per share starting Q3 2026, with buyback capacity governed by Basel III requirements. Excess capital plus high ROTCE is the compounding formula.

## 4. Recent Results (Q2 2026, reported July 14, 2026)

- Net income $21.2B reported (up 41%); $16.9B excluding significant items (up 13%)
- EPS $7.70 reported; $6.14 excluding items, vs consensus near $5.55
- Significant items: $4.6B net gain on Visa shares plus $1.0B equity investment gains
- Net revenue $58B, up 27% (15% ex items); NII $25.6B, up 10%
- Markets revenue $12.1B, up 35%; equities $6.0B, up 86%; IB fees $3.3B, up 30%
- Credit costs $2.5B; card net charge-off outlook lowered to about 3.2%
- AWM: net income $2.0B, AUM $5.1T (up 18%), client assets $7.7T (up 19%)
- FY26 guidance: total NII ~$105.5B (raised from ~$103B), adjusted expenses ~$107.5B (raised $2.5B)

## 5. Valuation

Banks are not DCF businesses; the standard framework is price to tangible book value justified by ROTCE versus cost of equity: justified P/TBV = (ROTCE - g) / (COE - g). With adjusted ROTCE of 23%, a 10 to 11% cost of equity, and modest growth, the model supports a substantial premium to tangible book. The stock trades near its 52-week high around $342, so the market already awards that premium. The valuation work is therefore about normalization: rebuild the earnings bridge stripping the Visa gain, haircut markets revenue toward a 3-year average, and see what ROTCE and fair P/TBV survive. A dividend discount model on the raised $1.65 quarterly dividend is a useful cross-check.

## 6. Risks

1. **Markets revenue mean reversion.** Management itself flagged that some of the trading strength may not repeat; an 86% equities jump is not a run rate.
2. **Rate cuts compress NII.** The $105.5B guidance leans on deposit balances and higher rates; a faster easing cycle pulls it down.
3. **Credit normalization.** Card charge-offs near 3.2% are benign; a consumer downturn changes the reserve math quickly.
4. **Regulatory capital (Basel III) outcomes** could constrain buybacks.
5. **Succession.** Long-tenured CEO leadership is a franchise asset with a finite horizon.

## 7. Conclusion

JPMorgan is executing at a level that makes every competitor's investor day awkward, and the Q2 print showed both earnings engines running hot simultaneously. The tension is that the stock's premium already assumes much of this persists. My stance is neutral to constructive: exceptional franchise, full price, with the swing factor being whether normalized ROTCE settles above 20%. Watching Q3 for the first read on markets revenue normalization and the new dividend.
